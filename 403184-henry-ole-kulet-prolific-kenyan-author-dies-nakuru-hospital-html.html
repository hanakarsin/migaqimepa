Henry Ole Kulet: Prolific Kenyan author dies at Nakuru hospital

- Henry Ole Kulet was born at Enkare-Ngusur Narok county in 1946 to a pastoralist father who later on moved to Ilkiremisho

- The author enrolled in school and joined Egerton College where he studied farm management as advised by the white settlers then

- Ole Kulet quit his agriculture career in 1987 after which he wrote several books including high school set book, Blossoms of the Savannah

Renowned Kenyan novelist and creative writer Henry Ole Kulet has died.

The three-time winner of Jomo Kenyatta Prize for Literature had been receiving treatment at Mediheal Hospital in Nakuru before his demise.

The family confirmed the 75-year-old prolific writer with a stellar career fell ill a week ago after which he was rushed to the hospital where he breathed his last on Wednesday, February 17.

Early life

Kulet was born in Enkare-Ngusur in Narok county in 1946 and his father later on moved to a place known as Ilkiremisho to escape western education after the colonial government ordered all boys be taken to school.

He continued with studies and joined Egerton College and being a son of a pastoralist, he chose to study farm management.

History has it that the settlers encouraged boys of his age from pastoralist communities to study range management and animal husbandry so as to help them improve their livestock.

Ole Kulet would later be employed as an assistant manager with Kenya Farmers Association during which be wrote his first ever novel, Is it Possible?

Realising that he had other passion outside the farm management, he opted to retire in 1987 at the age of 41 when he had risen through the ranks to become a personnel executive.

Literary works

In his retirement, Ole Kulet wrote nine books including Blossoms of the Savannah and was awarded the Jomo Kenyatta Prize for Literature thrice.

Blossoms of the Savannah was selected as one of the compulsory set books in Kenyan secondary schools for English and literature study.

Through the novel, the author sought to addresses female genital mutilation and early marriages among his Maasai community.

Other books he authored in his lifetime include To Become a Man, The Hunter, Maisha ya Hatari, Daughter of Maa, Moran No More, Bandits of Kibi, Vanishing Herds and The Elephant Dance.
